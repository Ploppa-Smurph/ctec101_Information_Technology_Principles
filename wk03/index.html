2.2 - Key Terms

2.2.4 - Computer Facts

Table 1: Timeline of Important Events in Computer History

1890 - Hollerith's Tabulating Machine At the end of the 19th century, the population of the United States was rapidly growing. Because there were so many people, the 1880 census took seven years to compile, and it was predicted that the 1890 census would take nearly twice as long. To solve this problem, the government hired Herman Hollerith to devise a solution. Hollerith created an electromechanical tabulating machine to process the census data. It worked by punching data into paper cards, then inserting them into the machine. Inside the machine, small metal pins would pass through the holes into a vial of mercury, completing an electrical circuit. This in turn powered an electric motor, which turned the appropriate gear in the machine to keep track of the count. Using this machine, the entire census was compiled in two and a half years.
1944 - Harvard Mark 1 Completed Working together, engineers from Harvard University and IBM created the Harvard Mark 1, which at the time was the most powerful computing machine ever built. It was fifty feet long, weighed five tons, and was built from about 750,000 individual mechanical parts. The Mark 1 was used by the Allies during World War II and assisted in creating simulations for the Manhattan Project. It could perform three additions or subtractions every second and one multiplication every six seconds.
1947 - First Transistor Developed Early computers sent signals via mechanical relays. Relays were metal switches that would physically move to make and break electrical circuits, representing 1s and 0s. Because the parts had to physically move, they were slow and tended to wear down over time. In 1947, scientists at Bell Laboratories invented the transistor. Transistors contain silicon, a semiconductor that can be made to sometimes conduct electricity and sometimes not. Because they didn't have any moving parts, silicon transistors were smaller, faster, cheaper, and more durable than mechanical relays.
1959 - Invention of the Microchip Throughout the 1950s, computers were large and expensive. Most computers were owned by governments, universities, and businesses - they had no place in the typical home. Then, in 1959, researchers at Texas Instruments developed an integrated circuit, or microchip. This new technology allowed an entire computer to be built into a single board, rather than needing large modules for each part of the computer. Microchips paved the way for personal computer ownership.
1977 - The Personal Computer Revolution In 1977, three successful personal computers were released to the public: The Apple II, the TRS-80 Model 1, and the Commodore Pet 2001. These three computers became known as the "1977 trinity." 1977 also saw the release of the Atari 2600, the first popular home video game console. For the first time, computers were marketed to the general public rather than just businesses and hobbyists. Four years later, IBM followed up with the IBM PC, giving birth to the PC/Apple rivalry that still exists today.
1994 - The World Wide Web Personal computer ownership began to skyrocket in the mid-nineties with the advent and rise of the World Wide Web, which began in 1991. In 1994, the web became public, and the world got a lot smaller. With new technology like email and chatrooms, computers became a viable way of communicating with other people and thus became more popular. In the early 2000s, social media sites like MySpace and Facebook were launched.
2007 - Smart Phones Through the mid 2000s, Internet access was mostly limited to computers and laptops. Then, in 2007, Apple unveiled the iPhone, the world's first smartphone. Now, people could access the Internet anywhere. Smartphones are now extremely common in the developed world, almost completely replacing other kinds of personal telephones.

2.2.6 - Human-Computer Interaction

Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) is a field of study that looks at how computers and other technology interact with humans.
Focus on User-Centered Design (UCD), a process where the Design team takes on the needs of the end user at every phase of the process. UCD products are as accessible as possible.
HCI also informs User Interface (UI) which is a design process that concentrates mainly on the look or style of a program. UI seeks to create a visual experience for the user that will be simple to use, enjoyable to look at, and meaningful.
ex: a software company is developing a word processing program - they would need to think about an interface style that was simple and intuitive while still offering the features that users are looking for. you need editing tools, multimedia support, and secure backups.
the development team will take into account the details like making certain the interface isn't overwhelming and that fonts are large enough.

the Questions of Human-Computer Interaction

1. Why are people using computers? what motivates them and what do they value?

2. What do people want to do with computers? what functionality and features do they need?

3. How are people using computers? how can they access what they want? what sort of aesthetics do they expect?

Principles for Measuring Human-Computer Interaction
  • Usability - refers to how suitable the computer is for the task and how easy it is to learn
  • Effectiveness - what percentage of a goal the computer is able to achieve. How good is the computer at doing the task that needs to be accomplished?
  • Efficiency - how much time it takes the computer to complete a task. also looks at how much time the user has to take correcting errors.
  • Satisfaction - how happy the user is with how the computer functioned. was the user satisfied with each step of the process?

2.4 - Media Formatting

covering: how pictures and sounds are encoded as ones and zeros; raster and vector images; when is file size more important, when is quality more important?

KEY TERMS

  • Sample - part of a sound wave that is captured, measured, and assigned a numeric value during digital sound recording
  • Bit Depth - amount of memory used to store the numeric value of a sound sample during digital sound recording. 8-bit sound uses 1 byte to store the value of each sample. 16-bit uses 2 bytes per sample. the higher the bit depth, the better the sound, generally speaking.
  • Sampling Rate - the number of individual samples taken each second during digital recording. Measured in Hertz, higher the sample rate, the better the recording. most modern recordings are 44.1Hz.
  • Frame Rate - Number of images, or frames, that are taken each second during digital video recording, most digital videos average 30 FPS, with a push towards 60 FPS in gaming and high def
  • Resolution - number of pixels in the width and height of each frame in a digital video recording, the higher the resolution, the clearer the image.
  • Color Depth - amount of memory computer uses to display colors in a digital image or video. 8-bit color images use one byte of data for each pixel. 24-bit color uses 3 bytes for each pixel.
  • RGB Display - display on a computer monitor that is made up of sets of tiny red, green, and blue (RGB) lights, easch set combining to make a pixel. a pixe will display different colors depending on the brightness of each light
  • Raster Images - images stored by dividing a picture into rows of pixels and collecting the color values of each pixel, in order, row-by-row
  • Vector Images - images stored by mathematically describing the shapes in the image. a circle might be stored as a center point and the distance from the center point to the circle's edge; a square will be saved as 4 lines each with a beginning point and a length.
  • File Compression - way to reduce the size of files making them easier to store
  • Lossless Compression - compression algorithm, such as GIF, PNG, PDF, and ZIP, that doesn't affect the original data.
  • Lossy Compression - compression algorithm, such as JPG, MP3, MP4 that slightly alter the data to make it easier to store, taking advantage of the limitations of human sight and hearing, and is usually used for images, videos, and audio files
  • Run-Length Encoding - common lossless compression algorithm that looks for repeated patterns of bits or bytes and records the pattern and then number of times the pattern is repeated
  • Compression Artifact - patch of pixels in a compress image or video that have less visable detail than the rest of the image
  • Bitrate - Kilobits of audio data processed each second
  • Video Codecs - Pieces of software that either compress or decompress digital media
  • Wireframes - first step in designing 3D model which provides the outline of the model, including only the vertices and lines without including and surfaces, textures, or lighting
  • Rendering - second step in designing a 3D model which converts the models int 2D images on a computer
  • Ray Tracing - advanced rendering method that is used to create incredibly realistic lighting effects for things like video games and movies

Run-Length Encoding is a Lossless compression Algorithm and makes no changes to the original file. Zip, PDF, PNG, and GIF are all examples of Lossless Compression. The

2.4.7 - Digital Images

Raster and Vector Graphics - when all 3 lights of a pixel (Red, Green, Blue) [RGB] are on (set to 255) then the pixel displays white.
Raster - these are BitMap images and are the most common. Raster images save every pixel of information and can be very detailed, therefore very large in size. they also do not scale up well. Small raster images will appear pixelated when scaled up.
Vector - 3D movies and video games use vector graphics (so do logos), as well as fonts. Instead of storing each pixel of information, vector graphics draw using circles and curves. Vector graphics are highly scalable and do not lose detail when stretched or scaled up.
The filesize is normally samller than raster images, making vector files easier to store. Vector graphics are not as easy to create as raster images and vector images do not include the same level of detail as rater images, most only contain a few colors and shapes.

2.4.9 - Digital Image Formats


.jpg - JPEG (Joint Photograhic Experts Group) is the most common. Compressed file type that reduces size 20:1 without sacrificing too much image quality (lossy). Supports 24-bit color. Easy to share and popular.
.png - Portable Network Graphics is a raster file format. Lossless compression of images. Released in 1994 to improve GIF format. Supports 24-bit color and an 'alpha channel' that allows transparency levels in an image. PNG cannot be animated like GIF.

.tiff - Tagged Image Fiie Format, created in 1986 for storing raster graphics. Created by Aldus (later Adobe), Microsoft, and Hewlett-Packard. TIFF has NO compression and is the ideal format for high-quality printing. Modern web browsers DO NOT support the format without plugins.

2.4.10 - 3D Models

3D models are used in movies and video games. They are used by manufacturers to create new products before production, and the can be used to 3D print solid objects.

Wireframes - 1st step in 3D modeling, provides outline of model, including vertices and lines without including any surface, texture, or lighting.
wireframes are skeletons. The models are created by connecting points (known as vertices) with lines (known as paths). These lines create 3-Dimensional polygons.
Wireframes are easier for computers to manipulate, so animators create the wireframe and then fully render the model with more powerful computers later.

Rendering - the 2nd step of the process is rendering, or taking a 3D model and converting it to a 2D image on the computer. Images are rendered for stills, or for animated video. Rendering can take a great deal of time and computer resources.

Ray Tracing - this is an advanced rendering method used to create realistic lighting effects for video games and movies.
